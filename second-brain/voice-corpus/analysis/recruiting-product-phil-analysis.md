## Phil's Hiring Philosophy

### The Archetype He Seeks
Phil is looking for a very specific person he openly calls a "unicorn":
- **Former software engineer turned PM** -- the single strongest signal. "I am looking for people who have been software engineers in the past, and not 'business analyst turned scrum master turned PM' (prob the worst path to get to talk to me)"
- **65/35 tech-to-PM weight** -- tech depth matters more than PM polish
- **Builder proof required** -- GitHub repos, personal sites, shipped side projects, conference talks. Resumes alone are insufficient: "without giving me proofs like projects that I can look at (github repo, website etc)"
- **AI-native** -- not AI-washed. Candidates must demonstrate they've already adapted to the AI era on their own initiative. "I want to find those who have already learned on their own about the change and/or can show me (extra curricular projects)"
- **Polymath** -- design + code + product + strategy in one person

### Hard Red Flags
Phil has a clear, explicit rejection taxonomy:
1. **Microsoft/Amazon background** (except Xbox) -- "MSFT is a trillion+ company shipping very bad enterprise products"
2. **Agile/SAFe certifications** -- "Agile/SaFE is a red flag"
3. **Six Sigma** -- "6 sigma such a red flag"
4. **Program management masquerading as PM** -- "Program management is totally not the profile I am looking for"
5. **QA-to-PM path** -- "QA reinventing themselves into product"
6. **Excessive turnover** -- "less than a year at each consecutive position feels excessive"
7. **AI-washed resumes** -- resumes rewritten with AI buzzwords but no proof of actual AI usage
8. **Failed solopreneurs** -- "looking at their idea you already know they were going nowhere, so that tells you a lot about the product 'intuition, flair and taste'"

### Strong Green Flags
1. Former SWE with real shipping history
2. Personal website or active GitHub
3. Side projects, especially AI-built tools
4. Gaming industry background (Zynga, EA -- indicates builder culture)
5. Deep technical PM at infrastructure companies
6. Co-founders who actually built the product
7. Teaching/writing about PM craft (Maven, O'Reilly, blogs)

### Task Design Philosophy
Phil is unusually innovative in assessment design:
- **"Reverse PM" task** -- gives candidates real code, PRDs, and artifacts; asks them to reverse-engineer the product and improve it. Novel and signals how someone thinks about existing systems.
- **AI-mandatory tasks** -- "I have built it purposely to be impossible to get done in 24 hours without AI." The task IS the AI screen.
- **Time calibration** -- "a smart candidate who knows how to use AI to their advantage can do this in less than 2 hours for a very convincing result"
- **Different tasks for different roles** -- data PM vs AI PM assessments are deliberately different

## Communication Patterns

### With Recruiter (Anthony)
- **Peer energy, high trust.** Phil treats Anthony as a collaborator, not a service provider. He builds tools for him, shares sourcing research, and riffs on ideas together.
- **Direct and fast.** Decisions come in short bursts: "YES - I'd like to talk to Yasha and Liam", "heads up -- I LOVED LEO", "def not a match"
- **Generous with context.** When rejecting candidates, Phil always explains why -- not just "no" but a teaching moment: "Others are program managers et al rebranding themselves as product managers."
- **Protective of his time.** Consistent pattern of pushing interviews to Wed-Fri to protect Mon-Tue "flowcus" time.
- **Humor as bonding.** GIFs, jokes about taxes, "you deserve a medal," "hunters of the elusive unicorn PM."

### About Candidates
- **Visceral initial reactions.** "I LOVED LEO -- pushed all my buttons all at once" / "Rohan's prez wasn't good at all" / "they have no chance"
- **But rigorous in formal assessment.** Initial gut gets validated (or reversed) by structured evaluation. Leo went from "LOVED" to "bombed" after the panel.
- **Specific about what impressed him.** Not vague praise -- cites exact signals: "he showed me his business (Meadow) he started with an engineering partner"
- **Honest about uncertainty.** "I am a little bit torn" on Aish. Doesn't pretend to have clarity when he doesn't.

### With HR (Victoria)
- **Respects her expertise.** Defers on comp, titles, process guardrails.
- **Pushes back on policy.** "we're making a big deal of 'offshoring' yet we can't hire next door?"
- **Engages on strategy.** Long exchanges about PM role evolution, PM/eng boundaries, and what "Builder" means.

## Recurring Themes

### 1. The PM Role Is Being Reinvented
Phil sees himself at the frontier: "the world is already moving fast past ai agent into claw-like ai agent swarms, the job evolving super fast into ai job queues and workflow management, agile is being dismantled." He's not just hiring for today's role but for where PM is headed.

### 2. Builder > Manager
The word "builder" appears throughout. Phil's mental model: the best PMs are people who build things, not people who manage processes. "For me titles are all 'builder' now."

### 3. Proof of Work Over Resume Polish
Consistent pattern of wanting to see artifacts: shipped apps, GitHub repos, personal sites, conference talks, blog posts. Resumes are insufficient and increasingly gamed: "ai-washed resumes" are a core frustration.

### 4. Speed With Rigor
Phil moves fast -- "now now now" -- but never cuts corners on evaluation. Full reports on every candidate, structured panel processes, multi-stage assessments.

### 5. AI as Hiring Filter
The take-home tasks are deliberately designed to require AI tools. This is both a practical assessment ("can you leverage AI?") and a philosophical filter ("have you already adapted?").

### 6. Recruiting as a Frontier Practice
Phil builds AI sourcing skills, shares them with the recruiter, riffs on "reinventing the resume" and creating the "farelli-mora model." He treats recruiting itself as a product to be innovated on.

## Voice Signature in This Domain

Phil's recruiting voice is characterized by:
1. **Enthusiasm that is earned, not performative.** He celebrates genuinely but only after seeing real evidence.
2. **Blunt assessments delivered casually.** Bad news comes in the same tone as good news -- direct, no softening.
3. **Teaching through rejection.** Every "no" comes with a reason that helps the recruiter calibrate.
4. **Humor woven into urgency.** Even when things are time-critical, there's a joke or a GIF.
5. **First-person accountability.** "it's on me", "I will write all the reports", "my apologies".
6. **Systems thinking about people.** Candidates aren't just good/bad -- they fit or don't fit specific team dynamics ("hard for her to navigate Andrey's team").
